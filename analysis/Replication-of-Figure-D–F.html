The scatter plot shows a strong positive correlation between TEAD4 and TAZ binding signals across genomic regions, with an R² of 0.67, indicating that approximately 67% of the variance in TAZ signal is explained by TEAD4 signal. Both axes are on a log scale, and the linear trend holds consistently across the full range of signal intensities, suggesting that regions with high TEAD4 occupancy tend to also exhibit high TAZ binding. The spread around the regression line reflects some degree of independent binding activity — there are regions where TAZ and TEAD4 signals diverge — but the overall correlation is robust. This is consistent with the known functional relationship between TAZ and TEAD4, where TAZ acts as a transcriptional co-activator that interacts directly with TEAD family members to co-occupy regulatory elements genome-wide.

The stacked bar plot illustrates the distribution of TAZ, TEAD4, and YAP binding sites relative to the nearest transcription start site (TSS), categorized across four distance bins. Across all three factors, the majority of binding sites fall within the 10–100kb range, indicating a predominant association with distal regulatory elements such as enhancers rather than proximal promoter regions. Binding within 1kb of the TSS, reflective of direct promoter occupancy, accounts for approximately 15–20% of sites for each factor, with TAZ and TEAD4 showing marginally greater proximal binding than YAP. Notably, YAP exhibits a larger proportion of sites beyond 100kb from the nearest TSS, suggesting a greater tendency to occupy distal or intergenic regions relative to TAZ and TEAD4. Overall, while the three factors share broadly similar genomic distribution profiles — consistent with their known co-occupancy — these subtle differences in distal binding may point to factor-specific regulatory functions beyond their shared roles in the Hippo signaling pathway.
